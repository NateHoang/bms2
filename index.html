    • Cell Envelope

    Component Detailed Description and Functions
    A) Cell membrane Phospholipid bilayer.
    • Contains carrier lipids, penicillin-binding proteins (imp. for cell wall biosynthesis) and enzymes.
    • Allows the active transport of nutrients.
    • Site of action for certain antibiotics.
    • Imp. for lipid synthesis, respiration and photosynthesis.
    B) Cell wall Confers Gram-staining characters, induce the host immune response, protection and maintain bacterial shape.
    1- Gram-positive bacteria cell wall The cell wall is composed:
    • Thick peptidoglycan layer. amino acid + glucose crosslinked.
    • Lipoprotein and polysaccharide polymers (teichuronic acid, Teichoic acids or lipoteichoic acids).
    • Lipoteichoic Acids and Teichoic acid are unique to G +ve. Contribute to the structural integrity of G +ve bacteria. Important in pathogenesis (adhesion to the host and avoidance of immune surveillance).
    2- Gram-negative bacteria cell wall Inner + outer membrane
    • Inner membrane: thin layer of peptidoglycan in the inner membrane.
    • Outer membrane: lipopolysaccharide (LPS; aka endotoxin) and proteins. LPS, unique to G -ve, include:
      • O-antigen: target for antibodies.
      • Lipid A, toxic component, is released from dying bacteria and it triggers pathogenic symptoms (including fever, inflammatory response, apoptosis and septic shock) via inducing TNF and IL-1.
    • Certain species may contain ẞ-lactamase enzyme between in the outer and inner membrane (several G -ve and Staph. "G+ve").
    C) Capsule Polysaccharide structure surrounding the cell wall. Present in some G+ and G-ve.
    • Protects the bacteria from phagocytosis (hence, increase virulence), and can play a role in bacterial adherence to the host cell.
    • Can serve as target for antibodies and may be used for making vaccines.

    Appendages

    Appendage Type Detailed Description and Function
    1. Flagella
  • Are protein appendages (flagellin protein)
  • They are used locomotion and contain prominent antigenic determinants.
    • 2. Pili or fimbriae
  • Composed of pilin protein
  • They facilitate bacterial adherence to the host cells and serve as virulence factors in certain bacterial species.
    • 3- Conjugation pili Rigid tubular structures used for DNA passage from one bacterial cell to the second.

    Bacterial Oxygen Requirement

    Category Detailed Description and Examples
    Metabolism Overview To generate ATP, bacteria can depend on respiratory (electron transport chain and oxygen) or fermentation (glucose & no oxygen) metabolism.
    1- Strict/obligate aerobes
  • Require oxygen for growth using respiratory metabolism, generating ROS (e.g: M. tuberculosis).
  • They contain superoxide dismutase, which protects the bacterial cells from the ROS. Catalase that convert hydrogen peroxide to water and oxygen.
    • 2- Strict/obligate anaerobes
  • Grow efficiently in very low oxygen concentrations <1%. Common in gut and mouth.
    • Example:
    • Actinomyces
    • Clostridium
    • Fusobacterium
    Fermentation is the metabolic pathway (glucose to lactic acid, alcohol or different metabolites).
    3- Facultative anaerobes
  • (with/without oxygen)
  • Most pathogenic bacteria are facultative anaerobes (Staph., Strep., E. coli)
  • Shift from a fermentative to a respiratory metabolism in the presence of air.
    • 4- Aerotolerant anaerobes Fermentative metabolism with and without oxygen.
    5- Microaerophiles Require the presence of small amounts of oxygen. E.g, Heliobacter sp. <21% but >1%

    Stages of pathogenicity

    Stage Description and Mechanisms
    1- Exposure Pathogen should overcome the host barriers and gain access to the host tissue.
  • Mucosal surfaces are important port of entry for microbes.
    • 2- Adhesion Pili or fimbriae or capsule facilitates bacterial adherence to the host cells.
  • Gram-negative bacteria membrane is rich with adhesins proteins.
  • Following adherence, bacteria can form biofilm for protection against antibiotics and host immune defenses.
    • 3- Invasion During invasion, pathogen aims to colonize the local tissue and evade the immune system. Several enzymes are secreted to aid in pathogenesis.
    • a- Collagenase: Degrade the extracellular matrix by degrading collagen.
    • b- Coagulase: converts fibrinogen to fibrin. The insoluble fibrin fibers can protect the bacterial from phagocytosis.
    • c- Immunoglobulin (Ig) proteases: IgA proteases inactivate IgA at the mucosal surface. S. pneumonia can cleave IgG heavy chains to decrease the immune response.
    4- Infection Successful multiplication of the pathogen leads to infection.
    • Local infection is confined to a small area.
    • Systemic infection refers to an infection that spreads in the whole body.
    5- Disease Transmission Occurs when the pathogen moves from one host to the second. Mucus membranes are common ports of transmission.

    Exotoxin

    Category Detailed Information from Lecture
    Definition and Composition Are the lipid A component of the G–ve bacteria (LPS).
    Outer membrane: lipopolysaccharide (LPS; aka endotoxin) and proteins. LPS, unique to G-ve, include:
    • O-antigen: target for antibodies.
    • Lipid A, toxic component, is released from dying bacteria.
    Pathogenic Effects Triggers pathogenic symptoms via inducing TNF and IL-1:
    • a. Induce the release of cytokines.
    • b. Increase vascular permeability.
    • c. Activate the innate immune response.
    • d. Cause fever, hypotension, disseminated intracellular coagulation, and shock.
    • e. Causing ischemic or hemorrhagic necrosis in various tissues.
    Core Characteristics
    • Release: Part of the cell wall. Released after cell death.
    • Production: Gram-negative only.
    • Antigenicity: Moderately or weakly antigenic.
    • Immunization: Can not be converted to toxoids.
    • Toxicity: Moderately toxic.
    • Fever: Usually produce fever.
    • Heat Stability: Heat stable, can withstand 60 °C heat for few hours.

    Endotoxins

    Exotoxin Mechanism Examples and Mechanisms
    1- Pore-forming exotoxin Disrupt the host cell membranes.
    • Staph. aureus secretes alpha toxin that disrupts the cell membrane.
    • Streptococcus pyogenes secrete streptolysin which create pores in the cell membrane.
    (2) Act as superantigens Cause massive release of cytokines.
    • S. aureus secrete toxic shock syndrome toxin (TSST-1) and enterotoxin superantigen that cause cytokine release.
    • S. pyogenes secrete the erythrogenic toxins A and C.
    • Those superantigens bind directly to MHC II, cause the recruitment of massive number of T cells and massive release of IL-2, TNFα and other inflammatory mediators leading to fever and shock.
    (3) Inhibit protein synthesis
    • Corynebacterium diphtheriae secrete diphtheria toxin can inhibit the protein translation.
    • Shigella dysenteriae secretes Shiga toxin that inactivates the 60S ribosomes.
    • Enterohemorrhagic E. coli secretes vero toxin that also inactivate the 60S ribosomal subunit and inhibit the protein synthesis.
    (4) Increased synthesis of cAMP Cholera toxin from Vibrio cholerae. Cholera toxin upregulates protein kinase activity, increase electrolyte imbalance leading to watery diarrhea.
    (5) Alter nerve impulse transmission “neurotoxin”
    • Clostridium tetani: secrete tetanus toxin. It induces spastic paralysis by interfering with the release GABA. Early symptoms include muscle rigidity, trismus, followed by sustained muscle rigidity and reflex spasm.
    • Clostridium botulinum: release botulinum toxin. Induce flaccid paralysis characterized by reduced muscle tone. Botulinum toxin inhibit the release of acetylcholine at cholinergic synapses in neuromuscular junctions and autonomic nervous system.

    The Five Pathogenic Types of E. coli

    Type Common Name / Disease Clinical Symptoms Pathogenesis / Mechanism Target Population / Habitat
    STEC (Shiga-like Toxin Producing) 0157:H7 strain; causes Hemolytic-Uremic Syndrome (HUS) Bloody diarrhea, abdominal cramps, vomiting, low-grade fever, jaundice, pale conjunctiva Vero toxin (Shiga-like) inactivates 60S ribosomes; damages glomeruli (clots/thrombocytopenia) and RBCs (hemolysis) General population; acquired via contaminated food/water

    EIEC (Enteroinvasive) Bloody Diarrhea (Shigella-like) Bloody diarrhea, severe abdominal pain, vomiting, and chills Mechanically damages and invades the intestinal epithelium General population
    EPEC (Enteropathogenic) Infantile Diarrhea Non-bloody watery diarrhea, severe dehydration, malnutrition, or stunted growth - Children under 2 years old
    UPEC (Uropathogenic) Urinary Tract Infections (UTI) Dysuria (painful urination), urinary frequency, flank pain Colonizes the vagina and urethra Females; most common cause of UTIs

    Key Summary of E. coli Diarrhea Types

    • Key Concept
      • Faculatative Gram-negative bacilli with capsule, catalase positive
      • Reside in human colon
      • It colonizes the vagina and uretha (can cause urinary tract infection)
      • Acquired during vaginal birth or fecal-oral route
      • Antigenicity: Used for classification of different subtypes
        • O Antigen (cell membrane)
        • H (flagella)
        • F(fimbria)
      Bloody Diarrhea: Look for STEC (associated with HUS and toxins) or EIEC (associated with direct tissue invasion).
    • Watery Diarrhea: Look for ETEC (Traveler's) or EPEC (Infants).

    Think of these types like specialized teams: STEC uses chemical warfare (toxins), EIEC uses hand-to-hand combat (invasion), and ETEC just opens the floodgates (watery diarrhea).

    Key Summary for Salmonella Identification

    1. Systemic vs. Local: S. typhi goes systemic (organs/blood), while S. enteritidis stays mostly local to the gut (enterocolitis).
    2. Capsule: S. typhi has a capsule (Vi antigen) to evade the immune system, whereas S. enteritidis is non-capsulated.
    3. Chronic Carrier State: S. typhi is famous for staying in the gallbladder, making the host a chronic carrier even after symptoms fade.

    Think of S. enteritidis as a "smash and grab" thief in the intestines, while S. typhi is an "undercover agent" that hides inside your own immune cells (macrophages) to travel through the whole body.

    • Integration: retroviral interrase enzyme allows integration of viral DNA into host DNA
    • Replication: Viral DNA replicated using host cell machinery to yield viral RNA and proteins
    • Assembly: Viral genetic material and capsid protein are assembled near membrane. Aspartate Protease leads cleavage of viral polyproteins and finally assembly of an infectious viral particle
    • Budding: Release of new viral molecules outside the cells
    Disease Course
    • Acute infection (Stage 1 or acute retroviral syndrome):
      • Within days after infection
      • Last for few weeks
      • ↑Virus titer
      • Flu-like symptoms (fever, fatigue, soar throat, night sweats) or GI symptoms
    • Chronic HIV infection (Stage 2):
      • Mostly asymptomatic, but can transfer disease
      • Latent stage that can last for decades
      • ↓T-cell
      • ↑Viral load
      • AIDS develop in this phase
      • Fevers, fatigue, weight loss, persistent lymphadenopathy
    • AIDS Stage (stage 3)
      • CD4<200/ul
      • Infected T cells killed by cytotoxic cells and natural killer cells
      • High viral load
      • Generalized lymphadenopathy, fever, weight loss, chronic diarrhea
      • Patient suffers from multiple opportunistic infection (fungal, bacterial, viral infections) and cancer
    		Highly active Anti-retroviral therapy (HAART) lifelong
